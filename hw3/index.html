Overview

In this project I built a physically-based path tracer from scratch. Part 1 handles ray generation and Moller-Trumbore intersection, Part 2 adds BVH acceleration to make rendering tractable, Part 3 implements direct lighting with both hemisphere and importance sampling, Part 4 extends to full global illumination with recursive bounces and Russian Roulette, and Part 5 adds adaptive sampling so we dont waste samples on pixels that have already converged. The coolest part was definitely seeing color bleeding show up once indirect lighting was working — the red/blue walls in the Cornell box actually tinting nearby surfaces felt really satisfying after all the math.

Part 1: Ray Generation and Scene Intersection

Walk through the ray generation and primitive intersection parts of the rendering pipeline.

In raytrace_pixel(), I generate num_samples camera rays per pixel. Each sample gets a random offset within the pixel from a grid sampler, which I convert to normalized [0,1] x [0,1] coords. These go into Camera::generate_ray(), which maps them onto a sensor plane at z=-1 in camera space — the plane spans from (-tan(hFov/2), -tan(vFov/2), -1) to (tan(hFov/2), tan(vFov/2), -1). The direction through this point gets normalized and transformed to world space via the c2w matrix. The ray starts at the camera position with min_t/max_t set to the clipping planes.

Each ray goes to est_radiance_global_illumination(), which uses the BVH to find the closest intersection. The intersection record stores the hit distance t, interpolated surface normal, and a BSDF pointer. All sample contributions get averaged into the pixel buffer.

Explain the triangle intersection algorithm you implemented in your own words.

I used the Moller-Trumbore algorithm, which solves for the ray parameter t and barycentric coords (u, v) simultaneously without computing the triangle's plane explicitly.

Given ray \( \mathbf{r}(t) = \mathbf{o} + t\mathbf{d} \) and triangle vertices \( \mathbf{p}_1, \mathbf{p}_2, \mathbf{p}_3 \), we compute edges \( \mathbf{e}_1 = \mathbf{p}_2 - \mathbf{p}_1 \), \( \mathbf{e}_2 = \mathbf{p}_3 - \mathbf{p}_1 \), then \( \mathbf{h} = \mathbf{d} \times \mathbf{e}_2 \) and determinant \( a = \mathbf{e}_1 \cdot \mathbf{h} \). If \( |a| \approx 0 \), the ray is parallel so we bail. Otherwise we get \( f = 1/a \), \( \mathbf{s} = \mathbf{o} - \mathbf{p}_1 \), and \( u = f(\mathbf{s} \cdot \mathbf{h}) \). If \( u \notin [0,1] \) we're outside the triangle. Then \( \mathbf{q} = \mathbf{s} \times \mathbf{e}_1 \), \( v = f(\mathbf{d} \cdot \mathbf{q}) \), check \( v \geq 0 \) and \( u + v \leq 1 \). Finally \( t = f(\mathbf{e}_2 \cdot \mathbf{q}) \) must be in \( [\text{min\_t}, \text{max\_t}] \). On a hit we update ray.max_t = t and interpolate vertex normals with weights \( (1-u-v, u, v) \) for smooth shading.

Part 2: Bounding Volume Hierarchy

Walk through your BVH construction algorithm. Explain the heuristic you chose for picking the splitting point.

The BVH is built recursively in construct_bvh(). First compute the bounding box of all primitives in range. If the count is <= max_leaf_size (4), make a leaf node and return.

For interior nodes I split along the axis of greatest extent — whichever of x/y/z the bounding box is widest in. The split point is the average centroid along that axis. I use std::partition to put primitives with centroid <= split on the left and the rest on the right (O(n) per level).

If all centroids end up on one side (degenerate case), I fallback to splitting at the median via std::nth_element to guarantee roughly equal children and prevent infinite recursion. Then recurse on both halves.

For traversal, has_intersection() does a slab-based AABB test and returns early on miss. Leaves test all primitives with short-circuit on first hit. intersect() is similar but checks both children to find the closest hit, using ray.max_t updates to prune far nodes.

Part 3: Direct Illumination

Walk through both implementations of the direct lighting function.

Hemisphere Sampling (estimate_direct_lighting_hemisphere): At each hit point we set up a local coordinate frame with the normal as Z. We take num_samples random directions uniformly over the hemisphere, transform each to world space, and shoot a shadow ray (with min_t = EPS_F). If the ray hits somthing, we grab its emission. Each sample contributes \( f(\omega_o, \omega_i) \cdot L_e \cdot \cos\theta_i / p(\omega_i) \) where the hemisphere PDF is \( 1/(2\pi) \). Average over all samples.

Importance Sampling (estimate_direct_lighting_importance): Instead of random directions, we sample directly toward each light source. For each light we take 1 sample (point lights) or ns_area_light samples (area lights). light->sample_L() gives us the radiance, direction, distance, and PDF. We check \( \cos\theta_i > 0 \) (light above surface), then cast a shadow ray with max_t = distToLight - EPS_F to check for blockers. If unoccluded, contribution is \( f \cdot L_i \cdot \cos\theta_i / \text{pdf} \). Average per light, sum across lights.

Compare the results between uniform hemisphere sampling and lighting sampling in a one-paragraph analysis.

Both methods are unbiased estimators of the same direct lighting integral, but they have very different variance. Hemisphere sampling shoots rays in random directions hoping to hit a light, but since the area light subtends a tiny solid angle, most samples return zero — so the image is noisy even at 64 spp. Importance sampling always points samples toward lights, so every sample contributes useful information. A 16-sample importance render looks way cleaner than a 64-sample hemisphere render. The difference is most obvious in soft shadows where hemisphere gives speckle and importance gives smooth gradients. Also importance sampling can handle point/delta lights with a single sample, while hemisphere sampling literally cant hit them (zero probability) and would render them as completley black.

Part 4: Global Illumination

Walk through your implementation of the indirect lighting function.

at_least_one_bounce_radiance() handles both direct and indirect lighting at a hit point. It sets up a local coord frame aligned to the surface normal, then checks the isAccumBounces flag: if true (default), it adds direct lighting via one_bounce_radiance() at every bounce; if false, it only adds the direct component at the terminal depth (r.depth == 1), so the output shows just that specific bounce number.

If r.depth <= 1 we've maxed out bounces so we return. Otherwise we apply Russian Roulette — continuation probability 0.65, termination 0.35. Per the spec, the first indirect bounce always happens (RR only kicks in when r.depth < max_ray_depth). Surviving paths get weighted by \( 1/\text{cpdf} \) to stay unbiased.

If we continue, we sample a bounce direction from the BSDF via sample_f(), convert to world space, create a new ray with min_t = EPS_F and depth = r.depth - 1. If it hits something, we recurse and weight the result by \( f \cdot \cos\theta_i / (\text{pdf} \cdot \text{weight}) \), where weight is 1.0 for the guaranteed first bounce and cpdf otherwise. When isAccumBounces is false, indirect replaces (not adds to) the output so only the deepest bounce shows.

est_radiance_global_illumination() just combines zero-bounce emission with the result of at_least_one_bounce_radiance().

Part 5: Adaptive Sampling

Explain adaptive sampling. Walk through your implementation of the adaptive sampling.

Instead of using a fixed sample count everywhere, adaptive sampling stops sampling a pixel once it's statistically converged. Flat well-lit walls converge fast and dont need many samples, while shadow edges and indirect-lit areas need more.

In raytrace_pixel() I track running sums \( s_1 = \sum x_k \) and \( s_2 = \sum x_k^2 \) of each sample's illuminance. Every samplesPerBatch (64) samples, I compute mean \( \mu = s_1/n \), variance \( \sigma^2 = (s_2 - s_1^2/n)/(n-1) \), and the convergence measure \( I = 1.96 \cdot \sigma / \sqrt{n} \). If \( I \leq \texttt{maxTolerance} \cdot \mu \) the pixel has converged and we stop. The 1.96 factor comes from the 95% confidence interval z-score — by CLT the sample mean is approxiamtely normal so this test is justified.
