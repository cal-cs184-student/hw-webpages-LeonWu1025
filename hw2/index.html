Section I: Bezier Curves and Surfaces

Part 1: Bezier Curves with 1D de Casteljau Subdivision

De Casteljau's algorithm evaluates a Bezier curve by recursively applying linear interpolation. Given \( n \) control points and a parameter \( t \), each step computes \( n-1 \) intermediate points using the formula: \[ p_i' = (1-t) \cdot p_i + t \cdot p_{i+1} \] This is repeated until only one point remains, which lies on the Bezier curve at parameter \( t \). I implemented this in BezierCurve::evaluateStep(), which takes a std::vector of 2D points and returns the next level of intermediate points. Each call performs exactly one step of the algorithm, computing one fewer point than the input via linear interpolation between consecutive pairs. The parameter \( t \) is accessed as a class member variable.

I created a custom Bezier curve with 6 control points (defined in bzc/curve3.bzc) for the screenshots below. The screenshots show each successive level of de Casteljau evaluation, from the original 6 control points down to the final evaluated point.

Part 2: Bezier Surfaces with Separable 1D de Casteljau

De Casteljau's algorithm extends to Bezier surfaces by applying it separably in two dimensions. Given an \( n \times n \) grid of control points and parameters \( (u, v) \), we first evaluate each row of \( n \) control points as a 1D Bezier curve at parameter \( u \) using the de Casteljau algorithm, producing \( n \) intermediate points (one per row). These \( n \) points roughly lie along a column and define another Bezier curve, which we then evaluate at parameter \( v \) to get the final surface point.

I implemented this across three functions:

  • BezierPatch::evaluateStep(): Performs one level of de Casteljau interpolation on a vector of 3D points at a given parameter \( t \). Very similar to the 2D version from Part 1, but operates on Vector3D and takes \( t \) as a function parameter rather than a class member.
  • BezierPatch::evaluate1D(): Fully evaluates a 1D Bezier curve by repeatedly calling evaluateStep() until a single point remains.
  • BezierPatch::evaluate(): Implements the separable approach — evaluates each row of control points at parameter \( u \) via evaluate1D(), then evaluates the resulting column of points at parameter \( v \).

Section II: Triangle Meshes and Half-Edge Data Structure

Part 3: Area-Weighted Vertex Normals

I implemented area-weighted vertex normals by iterating over all incident faces of a vertex using the half-edge traversal pattern h = h->twin()->next(). Starting from the vertex's outgoing half-edge, this pattern walks around the one-ring neighborhood by jumping to the twin half-edge (crossing to the adjacent face) and then advancing to the next half-edge (moving to the next outgoing edge of the vertex).

For each non-boundary face encountered during traversal, I retrieve the three vertex positions of the triangle and compute the cross product of two edge vectors: cross(p1 - p0, p2 - p0). The magnitude of this cross product equals twice the triangle's area, so it naturally provides an area-weighted contribution to the normal without requiring a separate area calculation. After accumulating the cross products from all incident faces, I normalize the resulting vector to produce a unit normal. This area-weighted approach ensures that larger triangles have a proportionally greater influence on the vertex normal, producing smoother and more accurate Phong shading.

Part 4: Edge Flip

I implemented edge flip by first collecting all existing mesh elements (6 half-edges, 4 vertices, 2 edges, 2 faces) around the two triangles sharing the edge. I drew detailed diagrams of the before and after configurations, carefully labeling every half-edge with its source vertex and direction.

For edge \( (b,c) \) shared by triangles \( (a,b,c) \) and \( (c,b,d) \), flipping rotates the edge to connect \( a \) and \( d \) instead, producing triangles \( (a,d,b) \) and \( (a,d,c) \). I used setNeighbors() to reassign all six half-edges' next, twin, vertex, edge, and face pointers. A key insight was that only the two half-edges on the flipped edge itself (h0 and h3) change their vertex assignment — h0 changes from \( b \rightarrow c \) to \( a \rightarrow d \), and h3 changes from \( c \rightarrow b \) to \( d \rightarrow a \). The four outer half-edges (h1, h2, h4, h5) keep their original vertex, twin, and edge pointers, but get reassigned to different faces and next pointers. I also updated all vertex and face halfedge pointers. The function returns immediately for boundary edges. No elements are created or deleted.

Debugging journey: My initial implementation incorrectly reassigned the vertex pointers of the outer half-edges (h1, h2, h4, h5) in addition to h0 and h3. This silently corrupted the twin relationships with external half-edges — for example, changing h4's vertex from \( b \) to \( d \) meant that h4 and its twin (an external halfedge) would both claim to originate from different vertices, breaking the edge. The mesh looked correct after a single flip, but the corrupted twin pointers caused crashes during subsequent operations like loop subdivision. The fix was to only modify the vertex assignments of h0 and h3, and limit changes to h1, h2, h4, h5 to just their next and face pointers.

Part 5: Edge Split

I implemented edge split by inserting a new vertex \( m \) at the midpoint of the selected edge, splitting the two adjacent triangles into four. Given edge \( (b,c) \) shared by triangles \( (a,b,c) \) and \( (c,b,d) \), the split creates vertex \( m \) at the midpoint of \( b \) and \( c \), and connects \( m \) to both opposing vertices \( a \) and \( d \).

This requires creating:

  • 1 new vertex (\( m \))
  • 3 new edges: \( m\text{-}a \), \( m\text{-}d \), and \( m\text{-}c \) (the right half of the original edge)
  • 2 new faces
  • 6 new half-edges (one pair per new edge)

The original edge \( e_0 \) is reused for the \( b\text{-}m \) segment. I carefully drew before-and-after diagrams labeling all half-edges with their source vertex and direction, then systematically set every pointer using setNeighbors(). The four resulting faces are: \( (b, m, a) \), \( (m, c, a) \), \( (m, b, d) \), and \( (m, d, c) \), all with consistent CCW winding order. I set isNew flags on the two new edges connecting \( m \) to the opposing vertices (\( e_{ma} \) and \( e_{md} \)) as true, while the two halves of the original edge (\( e_0 \) and \( e_{mc} \)) are marked false. The new vertex \( m \) is also marked as new. These flags are used by loop subdivision to determine which edges to flip. The function returns immediately for boundary edges.

Debugging journey: The trickiest part was ensuring that h3 (originally \( c \rightarrow b \)) is correctly reassigned to \( m \rightarrow b \) after the split, with its vertex changed from \( c \) to \( m \). An earlier version had h3 keeping vertex \( c \), which meant edge \( e_0 \) would have half-edges pointing from \( b \) and \( c \) instead of \( b \) and \( m \), corrupting the mesh topology. Similarly, the twin half-edge h11 needed to be assigned vertex \( d \) (for \( d \rightarrow m \)) rather than \( m \), to avoid both half-edges of an edge originating from the same vertex. Drawing detailed diagrams with every half-edge labeled was essential for catching these issues.

Part 6: Loop Subdivision for Mesh Upsampling

I implemented Loop subdivision in MeshResampler::upsample() following these steps:

  1. Reset flags: Clear all isNew flags on vertices and edges from any previous subdivision pass.
  2. Compute updated positions for original vertices: For each existing vertex, compute its new position using the Loop subdivision rule: \[ v_{\text{new}} = (1 - n \cdot u) \cdot v_{\text{original}} + u \cdot \sum_{\text{neighbors}} v_i \] where \( n \) is the vertex degree and \( u = 3/16 \) for degree-3 vertices, \( u = 3/(8n) \) otherwise. Store in Vertex::newPosition.
  3. Compute positions for new edge midpoint vertices: For each existing edge shared by vertices \( A, B \) with opposing vertices \( C, D \): \[ m_{\text{new}} = \frac{3}{8}(A + B) + \frac{1}{8}(C + D) \] Store in Edge::newPosition.
  4. Split every original edge: Iterate through original edges (using a count-based loop with safe iterator advancement to avoid invalidation) and split each one. The new vertex receives the precomputed Edge::newPosition.
  5. Flip new edges: Flip any edge marked isNew that connects an old vertex to a new vertex. This converts the split pattern into the correct 4-1 subdivision topology.
  6. Update positions: Copy Vertex::newPosition into Vertex::position for all vertices.

Extra credit — boundary handling: I added support for meshes with boundary. Boundary vertices use a simplified formula: \( v_{\text{new}} = \frac{3}{4} v_{\text{original}} + \frac{1}{8}(v_{\text{left}} + v_{\text{right}}) \), where \( v_{\text{left}} \) and \( v_{\text{right}} \) are the two boundary neighbor vertices. Boundary edges use simple midpoint averaging: \( m = (A + B) / 2 \). Boundary edges are skipped during the splitting step.

Observations on mesh behavior: After loop subdivision, sharp corners and edges become progressively smoothed because the weighted averaging formula pulls each vertex toward its neighbors. Features like the sharp corners of a cube or the edges of the teapot lid get rounded off with each iteration. Pre-splitting edges near sharp features gives the algorithm more local geometry to work with, which helps preserve sharpness by keeping the averaging more localized.

Cube Subdivision — Asymmetry

Loading dae/cube.dae and performing repeated loop subdivisions reveals that the cube becomes asymmetric. This happens because each face of the cube is triangulated with a single diagonal edge, creating vertices with unequal degrees. The two vertices at each diagonal's endpoints have higher degree than the other two corner vertices of that face. Since the Loop subdivision formula weights vertices differently based on degree, vertices with different degrees move different amounts, breaking the cube's rotational symmetry.

Cube Pre-Processing for Symmetric Subdivision

To fix the asymmetry, I pre-processed the cube by splitting the diagonal edge on each face, which creates a symmetric triangulation where every face has 4 triangles meeting at a central vertex. This gives all original cube corner vertices the same degree, so the Loop subdivision formula treats them equally. After pre-processing, repeated subdivisions produce a perfectly symmetric result.

Debugging journey: Getting loop subdivision working correctly required fixing bugs in both flipEdge() and splitEdge(). The initial flipEdge() incorrectly reassigned the vertex pointers of the outer half-edges, which silently corrupted the mesh. The first subdivision would look correct, but the second press of L would crash or produce no visible change because traversing the corrupted twin pointers during the second subdivision led to invalid memory access. Adding isNew flag resets at the beginning of upsample() and carefully guarding against boundary edges in the splitting loop were also important fixes.
